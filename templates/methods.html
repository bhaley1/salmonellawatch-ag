Data sources and update cadence

All cluster, isolate, and AMR data are sourced from NCBI Pathogen Detection (NCBI PD). NCBI PD is a public, FDA/CDC/USDA-collaborative platform that receives bacterial isolate genomes from labs around the world and runs daily SNP-based clustering to identify genomically related isolates.

SalmonellaWatch ingests three files per pathogen per release from the NCBI PD FTP site:

  • metadata.tsv — per-isolate metadata (host, source, geography, dates, assembly references, IFSAC category, food_origin, host_disease)
  • cluster_list.tsv — the per-cluster isolate membership manifest (Salmonella, STEC, and Campylobacter only; Salmonella's cluster info is delivered differently)
  • amr.metadata.tsv — AMRFinderPlus output (resistance gene calls per isolate)

The ingest runs nightly. NCBI itself updates files in place within a release accession, so even between Salmonella release bumps, individual files may pick up new isolates.

What a "cluster" is

A SNP cluster (NCBI PDS_acc, e.g. PDS000024682.215) is a group of bacterial isolates whose whole-genome sequences are genomically close enough to plausibly share a recent common contamination source. NCBI computes these clusters daily using a SNP-based whole-genome MLST approach with hierarchical clustering thresholds.

What clustering does NOT prove. Genomic relatedness is necessary but not sufficient evidence of a foodborne outbreak. Two isolates in the same cluster may share an outbreak source, may share a common environmental reservoir, or may simply both descend from a persistent clone that has been circulating for years. The cluster is a signal worth investigating, not a confirmed outbreak. Outbreak confirmation requires epidemiological investigation: case interviews, exposure histories, food sampling, and so on.

On the dashboard, "cluster card" and "active cluster" both refer to a cluster currently displayed: one with at least one human isolate whose collection_date is within the last 60 days.

Date semantics: collection_date vs. target_creation_date

Every isolate in NCBI has at least two dates and they can differ by months or years. Knowing which is which matters because most signals on the dashboard depend on one specific choice.

collection_date
When the sample was physically collected from the patient, food, animal, or environment. Reflects when illness or contamination occurred in the world. Used by SalmonellaWatch for the "New human cases in last 60d/30d/15d" filter and counts.
target_creation_date
When NCBI's pathogen detection pipeline first processed the isolate and added it to the cluster manifest. Reflects when the genomic surveillance system first "saw" the case. Used by SalmonellaWatch for the acceleration signal because that signal is asking "is this cluster being detected at an accelerating pace right now?"

These can diverge dramatically. A lab may collect samples in 2019, sit on them for years, and finally upload sequence data in 2026 — those will show recent target_creation_dates but old collection_dates. SalmonellaWatch's "new cases" framing intentionally uses collection_date so that actually-recent illness, not backlogged deposits, drives the headline number.

"New human cases" and the 60d / 30d / 15d windows

The headline "N new human cases in last 60d" counts cluster members with source_category = "Human" AND collection_date within the last 60 days. The 30d and 15d sub-counts apply progressively tighter windows.

A cluster appears on the dashboard if and only if it has at least one human case with a recent collection_date. Clusters that received new food or environmental isolates but no recent human cases are NOT shown, by design — the dashboard's purpose is to flag clusters where human illness is currently being detected.

The "Salmonella" choice of 60 days for the primary window is wider than you would use for Salmonella (where 30 days is typical). It reflects Salmonella's epidemiology — long incubation periods (up to 70 days for invasive disease) and slower laboratory characterization mean a tighter window would miss many cases that are still part of an active outbreak in epidemiological terms.

Geographic spread tiers

The cluster card may show one of four mutually exclusive geographic spread chips. They are computed from the canonical country and U.S. state of every isolate in the cluster (not just recent ones). Only the strongest applicable tier displays.

MULTI-STATE
≥2 distinct U.S. states are represented and no isolates from outside the U.S. Detected only for U.S.-only clusters because NCBI's geo_admin1 field is reliably populated for U.S. isolates but inconsistently for most other countries.
MULTI-COUNTRY
≥2 distinct countries on the same continent. A single-state U.S. cluster with one Mexican isolate triggers this, not multi-state.
MULTI-CONTINENT
≥2 distinct continents (out of North America, South America, Europe, Asia, Africa, Oceania). A cluster with isolates from the U.S. and Japan would trigger this even if only two countries are involved.
MULTI-HEMISPHERE
Isolates from both Northern and Southern hemispheres, classified by country population centroid. Indonesia is classified Southern; Tanzania Southern; Kenya Southern (by population centroid, despite crossing the equator); most other equator-straddling countries similarly assigned by population.

Caveat. Two-country clusters and 50-country clusters receive the same MULTI-COUNTRY chip. The label is a tier, not a strength-of-spread metric. To see the actual scope, hover the chip for the numeric counts (e.g., "12 countries · 4 continents · 8 US states"), or look at the map.

Country names are canonicalized to ISO names ("USA" → "United States"; "UK" → "United Kingdom"; etc.) before being compared. Countries not in the lookup table (~200 covered) are passed through unchanged and may not contribute to continent or hemisphere classification.

Import signal

The IMPORT SIGNAL chip is a pointer signal that the contaminated food vehicle may have crossed an international border before being consumed. In standard foodborne disease classification, an imported case is one in which the food vehicle traveled, not the person. Two ways this signal triggers:

  1. Country mismatch. The cluster contains human isolates from one country and food isolates from a different country. Example: U.S. human cases in the same cluster as Mexican food isolates suggests an imported food vector.
  2. food_origin field populated. NCBI's food_origin column, when filled in, records the country where the food was produced (separate from where the isolate was collected). Any mismatch between food_origin and geo_country triggers the signal.

What this signal does not establish. Co-occurrence of human and food isolates from different countries in a genomic cluster does not prove that imported food caused the human cases. The strain may simply circulate internationally. The chip is a pointer to clusters worth investigating, not a confirmation of imported-food etiology. Distinguishing import from non-import requires the standard public-health workflow: case interviews, food traceback, supplier investigation.

Relationship to the travel signal. A cluster may fire the IMPORT SIGNAL and the TRAVEL SIGNAL simultaneously, and they are not mutually exclusive. Each is computed independently from different NCBI metadata fields and answers a different question. See the travel signal section below for the distinction.

Travel signal

The TRAVEL SIGNAL chip is a pointer signal that the case-patient may have been exposed to the pathogen while traveling outside their country of residence. In standard foodborne disease classification, a travel-associated case is one in which the person traveled, not the food vehicle. The two surveillance bodies most cited in this dashboard's literature — CDC and ECDC — both treat "travel-associated" as a distinct case classification from "imported food vehicle".

The scientific distinction from import signal

Import (food vehicle crossed border)
The contaminated food was produced in country B, distributed to country A, and consumed by a case-patient who never left country A. Vehicle traveled; person did not.
Travel-associated (person crossed border)
The case-patient was exposed to the pathogen while in country B (typically through food consumed there) and was diagnosed after returning to country A. Person traveled; vehicle did not.

These are different epidemiological phenomena with different public-health responses. Import cases drive food recalls and trade investigations. Travel cases drive traveler advisories and surveillance of foreign food systems. The signals are not redundant.

How this signal is detected

The TRAVEL SIGNAL chip fires when at least one human cluster member's host_disease or isolation_source field contains submitter-annotated text indicating travel association. Recognized phrases (case-insensitive substring match):

  • "travel-associated", "travel associated", "travel-related", "travel related"
  • "traveler", "traveller", "returning traveler", "returning traveller"
  • "imported case", "imported salmonellosis", "imported infection"

Note that several of those keywords contain the word "imported" — these refer to an imported case (the person was infected abroad), not to imported food. The TRAVEL SIGNAL is still the correct chip semantically.

Important limitations

NCBI Pathogen Detection has no dedicated travel-history field. Travel association is detectable here only when the submitter happened to annotate it in free-text fields. The vast majority of travel-associated cases are NOT annotated this way — they appear as ordinary domestic cases.

This signal therefore has a very high false-negative rate. Treat the chip as "this cluster contains at least one known travel case worth investigating" — not as a measure of how travel-associated the cluster actually is. Absence of the chip does NOT mean the cluster is domestic.

Co-firing with import signal

A cluster can fire both the IMPORT SIGNAL and the TRAVEL SIGNAL at the same time, because they are computed independently from different NCBI metadata. Possible interpretations of co-firing:

  • Both genuinely apply. An imported food vehicle is causing domestic cases, and there are independent travel-associated cases linked to the food's country of origin. Genuinely informative pattern.
  • Only the import signal reflects reality. A travel-annotated isolate landed in a cluster whose true significance is an imported food vector unrelated to the traveler.
  • Only the travel signal reflects reality. The country mismatch driving the import signal may reflect that the strain circulates internationally rather than that any food was actually imported.
  • Both could be coincidental. The chips flag configurations worth investigating; they do not confirm either etiology.

Distinguishing these requires epidemiological case investigation — case interviews about food exposure history, food traceback, supplier-level investigation — which this dashboard cannot perform. The signals are pointer tools for surveillance professionals, not diagnostic tools.

Submitter diversity (multi-BioProject)

The MULTI-SUBMITTER chip fires when ≥5 distinct NCBI BioProject accessions contributed isolates to the cluster. A second tier ("very strong") is noted at ≥10 distinct BioProjects.

What this signal means

A BioProject (e.g., PRJNA001234) is NCBI's grouping for related sequence submissions, usually one organization's surveillance program or research study. When the same SNP cluster contains contributions from many distinct BioProjects, that indicates independent laboratories or organizations are each detecting related isolates — a much stronger surveillance signal than one lab repeatedly finding closely-related strains in their own collection (which could be cohort contamination or a single facility's persistent strain).

Caveat on this proxy

BioProject does not map 1:1 to "independent lab." Some large public- health agencies use a single BioProject for years of submissions (under-counting their independence); some studies span multiple BioProjects (over-counting). The signal is therefore a useful proxy for submitter diversity, not an exact measure. Interpret as "this cluster has caught the attention of many different submitters", not as a precise count of independent labs.

Human emergence signal

The EMERGING chip fires when a previously food- or environment-dominated cluster is now seeing human cases. The chip shows the percentage shift, e.g., "EMERGING (40% human, up from 5%)".

Epidemiological rationale

The canonical "outbreak emerging" pattern for foodborne pathogens is: a contaminated food source persists (often in a processing environment) and accumulates many food/environmental isolates over months or years. When that contamination finally causes detected human illness, the cluster's source-mix shifts from food-dominant to increasingly human-dominant. This shift is one of the earliest actionable surveillance signals — it indicates a known contamination source has now been linked to disease, and the source itself is often already identifiable in the food/environment isolates.

Detection criteria

The signal fires when ALL of the following hold:

  • The cluster has ≥10 baseline isolates older than 365 days
  • The cluster has ≥3 isolates collected in the last 365 days
  • The recent human-fraction is at least 30%
  • The recent human-fraction is at least 20 percentage points higher than the baseline human-fraction
  • The baseline was less than 50% human (otherwise this isn't "emergence" — the cluster was always mostly human)

Thresholds are calibrated to flag meaningful shifts (e.g., 5% → 30%) while ignoring small fluctuations (e.g., 40% → 45%).

Caveat

Like all signals on this dashboard, this is a pointer. A real investigation requires confirming the source-mix shift reflects a real change in case detection rather than a sampling artifact (e.g., a state suddenly increasing clinical surveillance).

Acceleration

The ACCELERATING (Nx) chip fires when a cluster is gaining new isolates faster than its historical baseline. Formally:

  1. The cluster must be at least 180 days old (by earliest target_creation_date). Younger clusters cannot form a meaningful baseline.
  2. Per-day rate over the last 60 days = n_recent / 60, where n_recent is the count of cluster members whose target_creation_date falls in the last 60 days.
  3. Per-day baseline rate = n_baseline / (age_days − 60), where n_baseline is the count of cluster members older than that.
  4. Chip fires when recent_rate / baseline_rate ≥ 2.0 AND n_recent ≥ 2. The "(2.5×)" number in the chip is the ratio.

Relationship to standard outbreak-detection algorithms

The general approach — flagging unusual increases in case counts relative to a baseline — is foundational to public-health surveillance and is implemented in mature, peer-reviewed algorithms such as CUSUM, EARS (CDC), Farrington (ECDC), and SaTScan. This dashboard's implementation is a simplified ratio, not a calibrated statistical test. Specifically:

  • The standard algorithms model expected count variance and provide a calibrated false-positive rate (e.g., flag at α=0.05). This implementation uses a fixed 2× threshold with no statistical calibration.
  • The standard algorithms account for seasonality. Salmonella has summer peaks; this implementation does not adjust for them.
  • The standard algorithms use guard bands to avoid baseline contamination by the very recent past. This implementation simply splits at 60 days.

For production surveillance, refer to the dedicated tools listed in the references. The chip on this dashboard is a "look at this cluster" pointer, not a substitute for proper outbreak detection methodology.

Why target_creation_date and not collection_date?

The acceleration signal is asking "is this cluster being detected by surveillance at an accelerating pace?" That maps to ingestion date, not sampling date. A lab dumping a long backlog into NCBI in one week can trigger this signal even when real-world illness is not actually accelerating. This is a known limitation; the chip should always be interpreted alongside the histogram to check the trend.

Collection-to-deposit lag

The "Deposit lag" field in the typing strip shows the median number of days between when cluster isolates were collected and when they were deposited in NCBI Pathogen Detection. Lower numbers mean the surveillance pipeline is delivering near-real-time information; higher numbers mean the data largely reflects backlogged deposits.

When the mean and median diverge by more than 30 days, the typing strip notes the mean as "skewed" — this indicates the cluster has a bimodal lag distribution (some recently-deposited fresh data plus some long-backlogged historical data).

How to interpret.

  • <30 days median: Active surveillance pipeline, deposits keeping up with collection.
  • 30–180 days median: Typical for academic and federal surveillance — sequencing and metadata review takes time.
  • >180 days median: The cluster's signal is heavily backlog-driven. Recent "new" deposits may reflect old cases. Acceleration signals on such clusters should be viewed skeptically.

MLST and Sequence Types

MLST (Multi-Locus Sequence Typing) classifies bacterial isolates by sequencing seven specific housekeeping genes and concatenating their allele numbers into a Sequence Type (ST). For Salmonella enterica, the loci are abcZ, bglA, cat, dapE, dat, ldh, and lhkA. The canonical reference database is PubMLST.

SalmonellaWatch picks one representative isolate per cluster (preferring Complete Genome > Chromosome > Scaffold > Contig, then highest contig N50, then earliest collection date), downloads its assembly from NCBI, and runs Torsten Seemann's mlst tool on it. Because cluster members are by definition genomically tight, we assume the resulting ST applies to all members of the cluster. In practice this holds for >99% of clusters.

The dashboard shows:

  • STn — e.g. ST6, ST121: the assigned sequence type.
  • "pending" — the cluster's representative has not been typed yet (MLST runs incrementally with a per-day budget).
  • "untypeable" — the genome failed MLST, typically because of poor assembly quality or unusual allele combinations not in the PubMLST scheme.

AMR genotype flag

The AMR chip fires when at least one isolate in the cluster carries an acquired antimicrobial resistance gene detected by AMRFinderPlus. The dashboard displays the AMR genotype strings as reported by NCBI Pathogen Detection, organized by country.

For Salmonella enterica, clinically important resistance includes genes conferring resistance to fluoroquinolones (e.g., gyrA point mutations, qnr genes), third-generation cephalosporins (e.g., blaCTX-M, blaCMY-2), and azithromycin (mph(A), erm(B)), as these are the primary treatment options for invasive salmonellosis. Multidrug resistance (MDR, resistance to ≥3 antibiotic classes) and extensively drug-resistant (XDR) phenotypes are of particular concern in serovars Typhi, Typhimurium, and Kentucky.

Caveat. The chip flags gene presence, not confirmed phenotype. Phenotypic confirmation requires antibiotic susceptibility testing in a clinical microbiology lab. The AMR genotypes shown are as reported by AMRFinderPlus via NCBI Pathogen Detection.

IFSAC food categorization

The IFSAC chip shows the most common food category among nonhuman isolates in the cluster, as recorded in NCBI's IFSAC_category field.

IFSAC (Interagency Food Safety Analytics Collaboration) is a federal interagency group — CDC, FDA, and USDA-FSIS — that publishes a standardized food categorization scheme for foodborne illness attribution. The canonical scheme is published by CDC IFSAC. Common categories include Dairy, Vegetable Row Crops, Meat-Poultry, Beef, Pork, Chicken, Turkey, Fish, and so on.

Human isolates are excluded from the IFSAC count — their IFSAC field, when populated, describes the patient's reported food exposure, which is a different epidemiological semantic from "isolate sourced from food category X."

Caveat. SalmonellaWatch displays whatever string the submitter entered. Inconsistencies in submitter labeling (e.g., "BEEF" vs. "beef" vs. "Beef products") will produce inconsistent category labels.

For clusters with nonhuman animal isolates, the "Animal hosts" line under Top sources of isolation shows the actual host strings as submitted to NCBI — e.g., Bos taurus (cattle), Sus scrofa (pig), Gallus gallus (chicken). Counts are the number of cluster isolates with each host string.

This information is hidden behind the broader "Animal" source category in most surveillance views. Surfacing it directly helps investigators quickly identify the species reservoir of a cluster.

Caveat

NCBI host strings are inconsistently submitted. The same species may appear as "Bos taurus", "bovine", "cattle", or "cow" depending on the submitter. This dashboard does not canonicalize — strings are displayed as submitted. When interpreting host counts, mentally collapse synonyms.

Geographic footprint map

The per-cluster map shows the cluster's full geographic footprint across all isolates with a known collection_date and a geocodable location. There is no time-window filter on the map (the "1-year window" was removed because Salmonella clusters span decades). Recency is encoded as dot opacity instead:

  • Most-recent isolate at the location is ≤365 days old → full opacity
  • Most-recent isolate is 1–5 years old → progressively faded (linear interpolation)
  • Most-recent isolate is >5 years old → minimum opacity (0.35)

Location aggregation: U.S. isolates with a known geo_admin1 (state) aggregate to state centroids; all other isolates aggregate to country centroids. Isolates with no geocodable location are dropped silently (not counted on the map).

Dot color: red = human cases at that location, teal = nonhuman, split red/teal = mixed location. Dot radius scales with sqrt(total count), clamped at a maximum.

Caveat on map outlines. The continent silhouettes are hand-drawn approximations, not Natural Earth-quality data. They provide geographic context at the small inset size; they are not cartographically precise. Small islands, archipelagos, and minor coastline features are omitted or compressed.

Cluster origins (oldest isolates)

The "Cluster origins" panel shows three reference points for when the cluster was first detected:

Oldest isolate (any source)
The earliest-collected isolate in the cluster regardless of source category. Tells you when the cluster's genomic signal first appeared at all.
Oldest human isolate
The earliest-collected human isolate. Tells you when this cluster first caused detected disease.
Oldest nonhuman isolate
The earliest-collected food, animal, or environmental isolate. Tells you when a (potential) source first showed up in surveillance.

Each panel displays the collection date, geographic location, and isolation source (when available), plus the PDT accession. Ties broken by preferring more-specific geography (admin1 over country) and then by alphabetical PDT.

Commodity-coded collection histograms

Each cluster card displays two stacked-bar histograms showing isolate collection frequency over time, color-coded by source commodity:

  • Collection frequency by year — all-time view showing how the cluster has grown
  • Collection frequency — last 12 months — monthly resolution for recent activity

Commodity categories are assigned by keyword matching against the isolate's isolation_source and ifsac_category fields:

  • ■ Human — clinical/human source
  • ■ Poultry — chicken, turkey, broiler, avian, egg
  • ■ Bovine — beef, cattle, cow, dairy
  • ■ Swine — pork, pig, hog
  • ■ Produce — vegetable, fruit, leafy greens, nuts, herbs
  • ■ Water — surface water, environmental water
  • ■ Other — all other non-human sources

Use the histograms alongside the acceleration chip: a cluster flagged as accelerating should show a visibly rising trend in recent bars. Acceleration without a visible trend may indicate a deposition artifact.

Serotypes of Concern (SOC)

Clusters whose consensus serotype matches a Serotype of Concern (SOC) are flagged with a ⚠ SOC badge. SOC designations follow the prioritization framework of Katz et al. (2024), which identified Salmonella serotypes of concern for reducing risk of salmonellosis using criteria including incidence, severity, outbreak potential, antimicrobial resistance, and association with specific food commodities.

The 21 serotypes classified as SOC in SalmonellaWatch-Ag are: Enteritidis, Typhimurium, I 4,[5],12:i:- (monophasic), Heidelberg, Infantis, Newport, Uganda, Braenderup, Muenchen, Montevideo, Javiana, Reading, Dublin, Oranienburg, Potsdam, Thompson, Saintpaul, Hadar, Schwarzengrund, Anatum, and Berta.

Katz TS, Harhay DM, Schmidt JW, Wheeler TL. “Identifying a list of Salmonella serotypes of concern to target for reducing risk of salmonellosis.” Front Microbiol. 2024 Feb 12;15:1307563. doi:10.3389/fmicb.2024.1307563

Top-level caveats

  1. SalmonellaWatch is a personal research project, not an official surveillance system. It is not authorized to issue health advisories, food recalls, or outbreak declarations. CDC, FDA, and USDA do that, with the benefit of epidemiological and laboratory investigation that this dashboard does not perform.
  2. Clusters are genomic predictions, not confirmed outbreaks. Genomic relatedness is necessary but not sufficient for outbreak confirmation. Cases in the same cluster may not share a real food vector.
  3. The CC and AMR labels have not been peer-reviewed. They are based on the author's compilation of public literature summaries. Treat them as pointers to the literature, not as authoritative biological claims. Domain experts should review before drawing conclusions.
  4. Travel association is under-detected. The travel signal catches only submitter-annotated travel cases. Most travel-associated illness is not annotated this way.
  5. Import and travel signals can co-fire on the same cluster. Each is computed independently from different metadata fields. Co-firing does not mean both phenomena are present — and absence does not rule them out. Distinguishing requires epidemiological case investigation that this dashboard cannot perform.
  6. Data lags real-time deposits by hours to days. The ingest runs nightly. A case sequenced today will appear here tomorrow at the earliest.
  7. Missing data is silent. An isolate without a collection_date doesn't appear in "new cases" counts; an isolate without a geocodable location doesn't appear on the map; an isolate without an IFSAC_category doesn't contribute to the IFSAC chip. These are common.
